Euler's Identity

\(\leftarrow\) Back to projects


We prove Euler's identity \(e^{i \pi} = -1\) using basic machinery.

Outline: We will show that \(e^x : \mathbb{C} \to \mathbb{C}\) is the unique function whose derivative is itself, and then show that when evaluated at \(i \pi\) it's value is \(-1\).

What is \(e\)? Euler's constant is given by \[e := \lim_{n \to \infty} (1 + \frac{1}{n})^n.\]

Claim: \(\frac{d}{dx} e^x = e^x\):

The derivative of \(e^x\) (denote it by \(G\)) at \(x\) is given by: \[\begin{align} G(x) &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{e^{x + h} - e^x}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{e^x(e^h - 1)}{h}\\ &= e^x \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{e^h - 1}{h}, \end{align}\] so in order to show that \(G(x) = e^x\) we just have to show that \[\lim_{ h \to 0} \frac{e^h - 1}{h} = 1\] (keeping in mind that \(h\) is a complex number).
