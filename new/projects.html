Carleson's Interpolation Theorem

A sequence \(z_n \in \mathbb{D}\) is called interpolating if for every other sequence \(a_n \in \ell^{\infty}(\mathbb{D})\) (that means there exists some \(M > 0\) which is greater than the magnitudes of every element of the sequence), there is some bounded analytic function \(f \in H^{\infty}(\mathbb{D}\) for which \(f(z_n) = a_n\) for all \(n\). This seems like a pretty strict condition, but Carleson (see, e.g., Garnett, "Bounded Analytic Functions") showed that the following are equivalent:
  • The sequence \(z_n\) is interpolating
  • \[\inf_n \prod_{m\neq n}\rho(z_n, z_m) > 0\]
  • \(\inf_{n, m} \rho(z_n, z_m) > 0\), and furthermore,\[\sum_{z_n \in S(I)} (1-z_n)\delta_{z_n} < C|I|,\] for all arcs \(I \subseteq \mathbb{T}\), where \(S(I) := \{re^{2 \pi i \theta} : e^{2 \pi i \theta} \in I, r \in (|I|, 1)\}\) is called a Carleson square and \(\delta_z\) is the unit point mass.
where \(\rho\) is the pseudohyperbolic metric \(\rho(z, w) := \left|\frac{z-w}{z\overline{w} - 1}\right|\). If you are familiar with the Blaschke map, you'll see this is the same thing.

Exposition

My group is looking at the sequence \(z_n = (1-\frac{1}{n^p})e^{2 \pi i n \alpha}\) and examining the relationship between \(\alpha\) and \(p\) when \(z_n\) is interpolating.

Some things to think about: An interpolating sequence must not have limit points in the domain, since by the identity theorem, any analytic function can be uniquely determined by it's values on a set with limit points.
Proof

Suppose two functions from an open set \(\Omega\) to \(\mathbb{C}\) given by \(f = \sum_{n \in \mathbb{N}} a_nx^n\) and \(g = \sum_{n \in \mathbb{N}} b_nx^n\) agree for all values of a sequence \((z_n)\) which converges to \(z_0\). Then consider \(h(z) := f-g(z + z_0))\), so that \(h: \{x - z_0: x \in \Omega\} \to \mathbb{C}\) vanishes at all values of a new sequence \((y_n\) which converges to \(0\).

Obviously \(h = \sum_{n \in \mathbb{N}} (a_n - b_n) x^n\) is now analytic, and we will show it must be zero. The crux of this proof relies on the idea that there must be some smallest nonzero term. Suppose that term is \(a_m - b_m\), that is, for all \(n < m\), \(a_n - b_n = 0\). Then we can write \[h(z) = (a_m-b_m)x^m(1 + j(z)),\] where \(j(z)\) is an analytic function. By continuity of \(j(z)\) and convergence of \((y_n)\) to zero, we can find some small \(y_k\) for which \(1 + j(y_k) \neq 0\). But neither is \((a_m - b_m)(y_k)^m\), so we get a contradiction because \(h(y_k)\) was supposed to be zero.

And analytic functions have to be continuous, because they are the uniform limit of continuous functions (polynomials) everywhere inside of \(\Omega\).



So far my group has found that \((z_n)\) is interpolating precisely when \(\alpha\) is Diophantine with exponent \(p\), that is, for all \(p, q \in \mathbb{Z}\) \[|p\alpha - q| \geq p^n.\]

Proof. First, we show that if \((z_n)\) is separated if and only if \(\alpha\) satisfies the Diophantine Condition. Then we show that when the Diophantine Condition is satisfied, the Carleson square property is also satisfied.

  1. If \((z_n)\) is separated then it satisfies the Diophantine Condition.
    Proof

    Suppose \(a_n\) is separated by \(c\). Then we will show that the condition (\(\alpha\) is Diophantine of exponent \(p\)) holds.
    Let \(r_n := 1 - \frac{1}{n^p}\). Computation tells us \begin{align*} c < \rho\left(\left(1-\frac{1}{n^p}\right)e^{2 \pi i k\alpha}, 1 - \frac{1}{(n+k)^p}\right) &\leq \frac{|r_n - r_{n+k}| + r_{n+k}|1 - e^{2 \pi i \alpha k}|}{|1 - r_n r_{n+k}e^{2 \pi i k\alpha}|}\\ &= \frac{\left|\frac{1}{(n+k)^p} - \frac{1}{n^p}\right| + r_{n+k}2|\sin(\pi \Vert \alpha k \Vert)|}{|1 - r_n r_{n+k}|}\\ &\leq \frac{\left|\frac{1}{(n+k)^p} - \frac{1}{n^p}\right| + C\Vert \alpha k\Vert}{|\frac{1}{n^p}|}\\ &= \left|1 - \frac{(n+k)^p}{n^p} + n^p C \Vert \alpha k \Vert \right|. \end{align*} Let \(n = mk\), and choose \(m\) such that \(1 - \left(\frac{m}{m+1}\right)^p < \frac{c}{2}\). Using the computations above we see that \begin{align*} \rho\left(\left(1-\frac{1}{n^p}\right)e^{2 \pi i k\alpha}, 1 - \frac{1}{(n+k)^p}\right)&<\left|1 - \frac{(n+k)^p}{n^p} + n^p C \Vert \alpha k \Vert \right| \\ &= \left| 1 - \frac{(m+1)^p}{m^p} + m^pk^p C \Vert \alpha k \Vert \right|\\ &< \frac{c}{2} + m^pk^p C\Vert \alpha k \Vert. \end{align*} Putting it all together gives \[\frac{1}{Cm^p}\left(c - \frac{c}{2}\right) < k^p \Vert \alpha k \Vert\] and we see that \[\inf_{k \in \mathbb{Z}^+} k^p \Vert \alpha k \Vert> 0,\] which is precisely the Diophantine condition.

  2. If the Diophantine condition holds, then \(a_n\) is separated.
    Proof

    \begin{align*} \rho\left(\left(1-\frac{1}{n^p}\right)e^{2 \pi i k\alpha}, 1 - \frac{1}{(n+k)^p}\right) &= \frac{|(r_n - r_{n+k}) + r_{n+k}(1 - e^{2 \pi i \alpha k})|}{|1 - r_n r_{n+k}e^{2 \pi i k\alpha}|}\\ &\geq \frac{|r_{n+k}(1 - e^{2 \pi i \alpha k})|}{|1 - r_n r_{n+k}e^{2 \pi i k\alpha}|} \end{align*} And as we can see, the denominator can be bounded above well \begin{align*} \left| 1 - r_nr_{n+k}e^{2 \pi i k \alpha} \right| &= \left|1 - e^{2 \pi i k\alpha} + \left(\frac{1}{(n+k)^p} + \frac{1}{n^p} - \frac{1}{(n+k)^pn^p} \right)e^{2 \pi i k \alpha} \right|\\ &\leq |1 - e^{2 \pi i k\alpha}| + \left|\frac{1}{(n+k)^p} + \frac{1}{n^p}- \frac{1}{(n+k)^pn^p} \right|\\ &\leq |1 - e^{2 \pi i k\alpha}| + \left|\frac{1}{(n+k)^p}r_{n} \right|\\ &< |1 - e^{2 \pi i k\alpha}| + \left| \frac{1}{k^p}r_{n+k} \right| \end{align*} since \(r_n < r_{n+k}\) while \(\frac{1}{a+b} < \frac{1}{a}\). Finally, putting it all together and assuming the Diophantine condition, we see that \begin{align*} \rho\left(\left(1-\frac{1}{n^p}\right)e^{2 \pi i k\alpha}, 1 - \frac{1}{(n+k)^p}\right) &> \frac{r_{n+k}\left|(1 - e^{2 \pi i k \alpha})\right|}{|1 - e^{2 \pi i k\alpha}| + \left| \frac{1}{k^p}r_{n+k} \right|} \\ &= \frac{r_{n+k}}{1 + \left|\frac{\frac{1}{k^p} r_{n+k}}{|1 - e^{2 \pi i k\alpha}|} \right|}\\ &> \frac{r_{n+k}}{1 + \left|\frac{\frac{1}{k^p} r_{n+k}}{\frac{C}{k^p}r_{n+k}} \right|}\\ &= \frac{Cr_{n+k}}{C+1} > \frac{C}{2C+ 2} > 0. \end{align*} since \(r_{n+k} > \frac{1}{2}\). (This makes sense because \(C\) is usually very small).

  3. Finally, we demonstrate the "Carleson Squares Condition", that is, for all arcs \(I \subseteq \mathbb{T}\) with length \(|I|\), we aim to show that \(\sum_{a_n \in S(I)}(1 - |a_n|)\), where \(S(I) := \{r e^{2 \pi i \theta} : \theta \in I, 1-|I| \leq r < 1\}\).
    Proof

    By our assumption, it takes at least \(\sqrt[p]{\frac{C}{|I|}}\) iterations of the sequence to arrive within \(|I|\) of where it started. Then \[\sum_{a_n \in S(I)} (1 - |a_n|) = \sum_{a_n \in S(I)} \frac{1}{n^p} \leq K\sum_n\frac{1}{\left(n\sqrt[p]{\frac{C}{|I|}}\right)^p} = \frac{|I|}{C'} \sum \frac{1}{n^p} \leq C'|I|.\] where the \(K\) which is absorbed by the \(C'\) comes from the Three-Gap problem. We want to show that if it takes at least \(m\) steps to arrive within \(I\) of the starting position the first time, then it will take at least \(C * m * n\) steps to arrive within \(I\) of the starting position the \(n\)th time.

    Lemma (Three-Gaps). Consider the sequence \((a_n)\) with \(a_n = e^{2 \pi i n \alpha}\). After finitely many steps, there are at most three different distances between adjacent \(a_n\).

    Corollary. If it takes at least \(m\) steps to arrive within \(I\) of the starting position the first time, then it will take at least \(C * m * n\) steps to arrive within \(I\) of the starting position the \(n\)th time.

    Proof of Corollary. We know it takes a finite amount of time for the largest possible adjacent distance to be less than \(|I|\) by 3 gaps theorem. Since this angular distance (call it \(\theta\)) is nonzero by assumption, it will never take more than \(\lceil \frac{1}{\theta} \rceil * (\text{number of moves it takes change your distance by exactly }\theta)\) turns to end up at most one turn away from ending up back in I. This is our constant \(C\).

    After \(C*m\) steps, we will have entered the interval at least \(m\) times (probably more). Thus it is proven, and setting \(K\) to be \(C\) in the original proof finishes the proof that the Diophantine Condition is necessary and sufficient for characterizing an interpolating sequence.
